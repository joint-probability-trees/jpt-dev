\title{\textbf{Ensemble Joint Probability Trees:\\[2pt]
A Likelihood-Geometric Account of Bagging and Boosting\\
for Hybrid Density Trees}}

\author{Joint Probability Trees Project}
\date{Draft --- Phase 0 (theory) for review}

\begin{document}
\maketitle

\begin{abstract}
Joint Probability Trees (JPTs) are tree-structured estimators of a
\emph{full joint density} over mixed numeric, integer and symbolic
variables, trained once and queried for arbitrary conditionals,
marginals, MPE states and likelihoods. A single JPT is a low-variance
but high-bias estimator: empirically it trails additive gradient-boosted
ensembles on point prediction. This paper develops the theory of
\emph{ensembles} of JPTs and answers one sharp question that decides the
software design: \emph{is there a single likelihood-boosting framework in
which discriminative gradient boosting is a special case of generative
density boosting?} We give a complete and, we argue, honest answer.
We first show that a JPT is a self-normalised, disjoint-support mixture,
so that any convex combination of JPTs is again a valid joint with no
partition function. We then prove that \emph{generative} likelihood
boosting---the additive mixture
$p_t=(1-\alpha_t)p_{t-1}+\alpha_t c_t$---is exactly Frank--Wolfe ascent of
the empirical joint log-likelihood over the convex set of densities, with
the canonical reweighting $w_i\propto 1/p_{t-1}(z_i)$ falling out as the
\emph{exact} linear-minimisation oracle and the mixing weight $\alpha_t$
as an exact one-dimensional concave line search. We then show that
classical gradient boosting is additive not in density space but in the
\emph{natural-parameter} (logit / conditional-mean) space, and that this
is precisely maximum \emph{conditional}-likelihood estimation: squared
error is a Gaussian conditional, log-loss a categorical one.
The two constructions are unified by a single meta-principle---greedy
functional ascent of a likelihood via a linear oracle on its functional
gradient---but live in two different geometries (the density simplex
vs.\ an affine natural-parameter space). We prove a \emph{tractability
dichotomy}: additive natural-parameter boosting of a \emph{joint} density
requires an intractable partition function, whereas conditioning collapses
that normaliser to a sum over the (low-dimensional) target alone, which is
exactly what makes discriminative boosting tractable; the generative
mixture sidesteps the normaliser altogether by mixing rather than tilting.
We conclude that discriminative \jptboost{} is \emph{not} a parametric
special case of generative \likeboost{}---a conditioned generative mixture
is a gated mixture-of-experts, not an additive-logit predictor---so the two
are best realised as sibling classes under a shared interface, with bagging
a third, non-greedy member. We close with the architectural consequences
and preliminary empirical evidence to be reproduced from the library API.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

A Joint Probability Tree \citep{nyga2023jpt} represents a joint
probability density $p(z)$, $z=(x,y)\in\X\times\Y$, by recursively
partitioning the variable space with axis-aligned, univariate splits and
placing a simple factored distribution in each leaf. Unlike a
discriminative decision tree or a gradient-boosted regressor, a JPT is
trained \emph{once} and then answers \emph{any} probabilistic query over
its variables: conditionals $p(y\mid x)$ for any choice of target $y$,
marginals under missing features, most-probable explanations, exact
likelihoods $p(z)$ for anomaly scoring, and i.i.d.\ sampling. This
``train once, query anything'' contract is the defining strength of the
formalism and the property every extension must be measured against.

The price of that generality is statistical: a single piecewise-constant
tree predicts the conditional mean or mode of the leaf a query routes to
and \emph{cannot fit its own residuals}. In a controlled benchmark against
LightGBM \citep{ke2017lightgbm}, a $200$-tree additive ensemble, a single
JPT trailed on $7$ of $8$ datasets, with the gap widest exactly where bias
dominates (\texttt{digits} accuracy $0.76$ vs.\ $0.98$; \texttt{california}
$R^2\,0.58$ vs.\ $0.81$). The natural remedy is to \emph{ensemble} JPTs.
But ``ensemble'' splits immediately into two philosophically different
constructions, and the split is the crux of this paper:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.4em]
\item \textbf{Generative ensembling} keeps every member a full joint and
combines them into a richer joint. Bagging (average the members) reduces
variance; \emph{likelihood boosting}---a greedy additive
\emph{mixture} of JPTs that up-weights the points the current mixture models
worst---reduces bias while remaining a bona-fide joint density.
\item \textbf{Discriminative ensembling} (\jptboost) runs the textbook
gradient-boosting recipe with JPTs as base learners on the residual /
softmax gradient. It is the larger accuracy lever, but each round models
the residual of \emph{one} target, so the ensemble estimates
$\E[y\mid x]$ and \emph{abandons the joint}.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{The central question.}
Both schemes are ``boosting'' and both, informally, ``improve a
likelihood.'' This invites a unification hypothesis that would simplify the
entire design: \emph{perhaps discriminative \jptboost{} is just generative
\likeboost{} applied to the conditional likelihood $p(y\mid x)$ instead of
the joint $p(x,y)$---one framework, one class, parameterised by an
objective.} If true, the library should ship a single boosting class with
an ``objective'' knob. If false, it should ship two related classes under a
shared interface. The mathematics, not taste, must decide. This is a
\emph{theory-first} (``Phase 0'') deliverable: we settle the question
before writing the ensemble code.

\paragraph{Contributions and findings.}
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.6em]
\item We formalise a JPT as a \emph{self-normalised disjoint-support
mixture} and prove that any convex combination of JPTs over a shared
variable domain is again a normalised joint, \emph{with no partition
function} (\S\ref{sec:jpt}, Prop.~\ref{prop:mixture-closed}).
\item We prove that generative likelihood boosting is \emph{exactly}
Frank--Wolfe ascent of the empirical joint log-likelihood over the convex
set of densities; the reweighting $w_i\propto 1/p_{t-1}(z_i)$ is the exact
linear oracle and $\alpha_t$ an exact concave line search
(\S\ref{sec:likeboost}, Thm.~\ref{thm:fw}). We give the $O(1/t)$
convergence statement.
\item We show classical gradient boosting is maximum
\emph{conditional}-likelihood estimation with an \emph{additive
natural parameter}: squared error $=$ Gaussian conditional, log-loss $=$
categorical conditional (\S\ref{sec:gbm}, Prop.~\ref{prop:gbm-mle}).
\item We reconcile the two notions of ``additive'' and prove a
\emph{tractability dichotomy} (Thm.~\ref{thm:dichotomy}): additive
natural-parameter boosting of a \emph{joint} is intractable (a
product-of-experts partition function over all of $(x,y)$); conditioning
collapses the normaliser to the target alone, which is what makes
discriminative boosting tractable; the generative mixture avoids the
normaliser entirely by mixing instead of tilting.
\item We settle the subsumption question \emph{in the negative}:
\jptboost{} is not a parametric special case of \likeboost{}; conditioning
a generative boosted mixture yields a gated mixture-of-experts predictor,
structurally distinct from---and generally weaker at point prediction
than---additive-logit GBM (\S\ref{sec:reconcile},
Props.~\ref{prop:nonsubsumption}--\ref{prop:moe}). The constructions are
unified only at the level of a meta-principle (greedy functional likelihood
ascent), which we make precise (Def.~\ref{def:metaboost}).
\item We translate the verdict into an API---a \mixture{} base, a
\jptforest{} (bagging), a generative \likeboost{} and a discriminative
\jptboost{}---and state the implementation invariants
(\S\ref{sec:arch}).
\end{enumerate}

\section{Background and notation}
\label{sec:background}

\paragraph{Data space.}
Let $\Z=\X\times\Y$ be the space of \emph{all} modelled variables, with a
fixed base (reference) measure $\mu$ on $\Z$---Lebesgue on numeric axes,
counting measure on symbolic / integer axes, products thereof on mixed
spaces. A \emph{density} is a measurable $p:\Z\to\R_{\ge 0}$ with
$\int_\Z p\, d\mu = 1$. Write $\Prob$ for the convex set of densities on
$\Z$ (w.r.t.\ $\mu$). Given a sample $D=\{z_i\}_{i=1}^n$, the empirical
mean log-likelihood of a density is
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:LL}
\mathcal{L}(p)\;=\;\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n \log p(z_i).
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Gradient boosting machines.}
A GBM \citep{friedman2001gbm} builds an additive predictor
$F_M=\sum_{m=1}^M \nu\, h_m$ in a function space $\mathcal{H}\subseteq
\{F:\X\to\R^K\}$, minimising an empirical risk
$R(F)=\frac1n\sum_i \ell(y_i,F(x_i))$ by stage-wise functional gradient
descent: at round $m$ a base learner $h_m$ is fit (in least squares) to the
negative gradient---the \emph{pseudo-residual}
$r_i=-\,\partial \ell(y_i,F(x_i))/\partial F$---and added with shrinkage
$\nu\in(0,1]$. We take JPTs (used as conditional-mean predictors) as the
base learners $h_m$.

\paragraph{Frank--Wolfe.}
The Frank--Wolfe (conditional gradient) algorithm
\citep{frankwolfe1956,jaggi2013fw} maximises a concave differentiable
objective $\mathcal{L}$ over a convex set $\mathcal{C}$ without projection:
given $p_{t-1}\in\mathcal{C}$ it calls a \emph{linear-minimisation oracle}
(LMO) $s_t=\argmax_{s\in\mathcal{C}}\inner{\nabla\mathcal{L}(p_{t-1})}{s}$
and steps $p_t=(1-\alpha_t)p_{t-1}+\alpha_t s_t$ with
$\alpha_t\in[0,1]$. Every iterate is a convex combination of atoms, hence
stays in $\mathcal{C}$.

\section{A JPT is a self-normalised disjoint-support mixture}
\label{sec:jpt}

A JPT over variables $Z$ induces a partition of $\Z$ into leaf cells. Each
internal node tests one variable against a threshold (numeric / integer) or
a value subset (symbolic); a leaf $\ell$ owns the cell $C_\ell\subseteq\Z$
of points whose decision path ends at $\ell$. The cells are disjoint and
cover $\Z$:
\[
C_\ell \cap C_{\ell'} = \emptyset \ (\ell\neq\ell'), \qquad
\bigcup_{\ell\in\Leaf} C_\ell = \Z .
\]
Crucially, the splits constrain a \emph{subset} of variables; the remaining
variables range freely within the cell. Each leaf carries a
\emph{factored} local density---a product of per-variable univariate
distributions (a na\"ive-Bayes factorisation \emph{within} the leaf),
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:leaf}
p_\ell(z) \;=\; \prod_{v\in Z} p_{\ell,v}(z_v),
\end{equation}
each factor a univariate density/mass restricted to its axis' cell
constraint and self-normalised there. The leaf prior $\pi_\ell$ is the
fraction of training mass routed to $\ell$ ($\sum_\ell \pi_\ell=1$). The
JPT density is the disjoint-support mixture
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:jpt}
p^{\jpt}(z) \;=\; \sum_{\ell\in\Leaf} \pi_\ell\, p_\ell(z),
\qquad \operatorname{supp} p_\ell \subseteq C_\ell .
\end{equation}

\begin{proposition}[Self-normalisation]
\label{prop:jpt-norm}
If each factor in \eqref{eq:leaf} integrates to $1$ over its axis cell and
$\sum_\ell \pi_\ell=1$, then $p^{\jpt}\in\Prob$.
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
By Fubini over the product axes, $\int_{C_\ell}p_\ell\,d\mu=\prod_v
\int p_{\ell,v}\,d\mu_v = 1$. The cells are disjoint, so
$\int_\Z p^{\jpt}\,d\mu = \sum_\ell \pi_\ell \int_{C_\ell}p_\ell\,d\mu
= \sum_\ell\pi_\ell = 1$. Non-negativity is inherited factorwise.
\end{proof}

The point of Proposition~\ref{prop:jpt-norm} for ensembling is that
normalisation is \emph{structural}, not the result of dividing by an
integral. This is what survives convex combination:

\begin{proposition}[Mixtures of JPTs are JP-valued joints]
\label{prop:mixture-closed}
Let $p^{(1)},\dots,p^{(M)}\in\Prob$ be JPTs over a common variable domain
$Z$ and base measure $\mu$, and let $w\in\Delta^{M-1}$ be a weight vector
on the simplex. Then
\[
\bar p(z)\;=\;\sum_{m=1}^M w_m\, p^{(m)}(z)
\]
is a density on $\Z$, and every linear query functional---marginalisation,
conditioning, expectation, likelihood---commutes with the mixing weights:
$\bar p(A)=\sum_m w_m p^{(m)}(A)$ for measurable $A$, and
$\E_{\bar p}[g]=\sum_m w_m \E_{p^{(m)}}[g]$ for integrable $g$. Sampling is
``pick $m\sim w$, then sample $z\sim p^{(m)}$.''
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
$\bar p\ge 0$ and $\int\bar p\,d\mu=\sum_m w_m=1$, so $\bar p\in\Prob$.
Marginals and expectations are integrals, hence linear in $p$; conditioning
$\bar p(y\mid x)=\bar p(x,y)/\bar p(x)$ is a ratio of two such linear
functionals. The sampling identity is the standard mixture sampler.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[No partition function]
\label{rem:no-Z}
Proposition~\ref{prop:mixture-closed} is the structural reason a
\emph{mixture} ensemble of JPTs is free of the normalisation cost that
sinks \emph{product}-style density combination. A product-of-experts
$\bar p\propto\prod_m (p^{(m)})^{\beta_m}$ requires the partition function
$Z=\int\prod_m (p^{(m)})^{\beta_m}d\mu$, an integral over all of $\Z$ with
no closed form for tree densities. A convex mixture has $Z\equiv 1$. Every
ensemble we build---bagging and generative boosting---is a mixture for
exactly this reason.
\end{remark}

Two query primitives recur and are worth naming, because the library
delegates to them. For a posterior or expectation over a target with a
mixture, the \emph{per-component} leaf distributions of the queried type
are merged by the type's own \texttt{merge} with the (evidence-adjusted)
component weights; for a likelihood the mixture density is the weighted sum
of component densities. Both are instances of
Proposition~\ref{prop:mixture-closed}.

\section{Bagging: the equal-weight mixture}
\label{sec:bagging}

The simplest ensemble fixes $w_m=1/M$ and trains each $p^{(m)}$ on an
independent bootstrap resample $D^{(m)}$ of $D$:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:bag}
p^{\jptforest}(z)\;=\;\frac1M\sum_{m=1}^M p^{(m)}(z).
\end{equation}
By Proposition~\ref{prop:mixture-closed} this is a valid joint and inherits
the full query surface. Its role is \emph{variance reduction}, not bias
reduction, and the mechanism is the classic one
\citep{breiman1996bagging,breiman2001rf}.

\begin{proposition}[Variance reduction of the bagged query]
\label{prop:bag-var}
Fix a query functional $T(p)=\E_p[g\mid x]$ (e.g.\ a leaf-routed
conditional mean) that is approximately linear in the leaf statistics, and
let $\hat T^{(m)}=T(p^{(m)})$ be the per-member estimates with common
variance $\sigma^2$ and average pairwise correlation $\rho$ across
bootstrap members. The bagged estimate
$\bar T=\frac1M\sum_m \hat T^{(m)}$ has variance
\[
\Var(\bar T)\;=\;\rho\,\sigma^2 \;+\; \frac{1-\rho}{M}\,\sigma^2
\;\xrightarrow[M\to\infty]{}\; \rho\,\sigma^2 .
\]
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
Standard: $\Var\!\big(\frac1M\sum_m \hat T^{(m)}\big)=\frac1{M^2}
\big(M\sigma^2 + M(M-1)\rho\sigma^2\big)=\rho\sigma^2+\frac{1-\rho}{M}
\sigma^2$.
\end{proof}

Bagging therefore removes the de-correlated part of the leaf-estimate
variance, which is large for high-variance greedy trees, but leaves the
common bias (and the correlated variance $\rho\sigma^2$) untouched. It is
the low-risk baseline: it strictly preserves the generative contract and
cannot, in expectation, increase bias. The remaining gap to additive
ensembles is the part bagging \emph{cannot} reach---bias---which is the
province of boosting.

\section{Generative likelihood boosting is Frank--Wolfe on the joint LL}
\label{sec:likeboost}

We now show the generative booster of the introduction is not a heuristic
but an exact instance of a textbook convex-optimisation algorithm. Recall
the additive-mixture update
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:boost-update}
p_t \;=\; (1-\alpha_t)\,p_{t-1} \;+\; \alpha_t\, c_t,
\qquad \alpha_t\in[0,1],\ \ c_t\in\mathcal{A},
\end{equation}
where $\mathcal{A}\subseteq\Prob$ is the set of JPTs trainable from
(reweighted) data---the available ``atoms.'' We maximise the empirical
joint log-likelihood $\mathcal{L}$ of \eqref{eq:LL} over the convex hull
$\mathcal{C}=\operatorname{conv}(\mathcal{A})$.

\begin{lemma}[Concavity and functional gradient]
\label{lem:grad}
$\mathcal{L}$ is concave on $\Prob$. Its directional derivative at $p$ in
the feasible direction $c-p$ ($c\in\Prob$) is
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:dirderiv}
D\mathcal{L}(p)[\,c-p\,]
\;=\;\frac1n\sum_{i=1}^n \frac{c(z_i)-p(z_i)}{p(z_i)}
\;=\;\Big(\tfrac1n\sum_{i=1}^n \tfrac{c(z_i)}{p(z_i)}\Big)-1 .
\end{equation}
Equivalently the functional gradient acts as
$\inner{\nabla\mathcal{L}(p)}{c}=\frac1n\sum_i c(z_i)/p(z_i)$.
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
$\log$ is concave and $p\mapsto\log p(z_i)$ is the composition with a
linear evaluation, hence concave; a sum of concaves is concave. For the
derivative, let $g(\alpha)=\mathcal{L}\big((1-\alpha)p+\alpha c\big)=
\frac1n\sum_i\log\big(p(z_i)+\alpha(c(z_i)-p(z_i))\big)$. Then
$g'(0)=\frac1n\sum_i (c(z_i)-p(z_i))/p(z_i)$, which is
\eqref{eq:dirderiv}.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[Likelihood boosting $=$ Frank--Wolfe]
\label{thm:fw}
The update \eqref{eq:boost-update} is one Frank--Wolfe iteration for
$\max_{p\in\mathcal{C}}\mathcal{L}(p)$ when:
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\roman*),leftmargin=2em]
\item \textbf{(Oracle $=$ inverse-density reweighting.)} The component
$c_t$ is chosen to maximise the weighted likelihood
\[
c_t \in \argmax_{c\in\mathcal{A}}\ \sum_{i=1}^n w_i\, c(z_i),
\qquad w_i=\frac{1}{n\,p_{t-1}(z_i)} ,
\]
i.e.\ $c_t$ puts mass where the current mixture is worst
($w_i\propto 1/p_{t-1}(z_i)$). This is exactly the FW LMO
$\argmax_{c}\inner{\nabla\mathcal{L}(p_{t-1})}{c}$.
\item \textbf{(Step $=$ exact line search.)} $\alpha_t=\argmax_{\alpha
\in[0,1]}g(\alpha)$ with $g(\alpha)=\mathcal{L}\big((1-\alpha)p_{t-1}+
\alpha c_t\big)$, a concave one-dimensional problem.
\end{enumerate}
Every iterate $p_t\in\mathcal{C}\subseteq\Prob$ is a normalised joint,
with no partition function (Rmk.~\ref{rem:no-Z}).
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
By Lemma~\ref{lem:grad} the LMO
$\argmax_{c}\inner{\nabla\mathcal{L}(p_{t-1})}{c}
=\argmax_c \frac1n\sum_i c(z_i)/p_{t-1}(z_i)
=\argmax_c\sum_i w_i c(z_i)$ with $w_i=1/(n p_{t-1}(z_i))$, which is
(i). The FW step $p_t=(1-\alpha_t)p_{t-1}+\alpha_t c_t$ is
\eqref{eq:boost-update}; exact line search is (ii). $g$ is concave by
Lemma~\ref{lem:grad} (restriction of a concave functional to a line), so
the line search is a concave 1-D maximisation. Convexity of $\mathcal{C}$
gives $p_t\in\mathcal{C}$; Proposition~\ref{prop:mixture-closed} gives
normalisation.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[Convergence]
\label{cor:rate}
Suppose densities in $\mathcal{C}$ are bounded below by $\eta>0$ on the
sample (enforced in practice by an $\eps$-floor on component
likelihoods), so $\mathcal{L}$ has curvature constant
$C_{\mathcal{L}}<\infty$ on $\mathcal{C}$. Then the Frank--Wolfe iterates of
Theorem~\ref{thm:fw} satisfy
\[
\mathcal{L}(p^\star)-\mathcal{L}(p_t)\;\le\;\frac{2\,C_{\mathcal{L}}}{t+2},
\qquad p^\star=\argmax_{p\in\mathcal{C}}\mathcal{L}(p),
\]
i.e.\ an $O(1/t)$ optimality gap; if the oracle is approximate to additive
accuracy $\delta$, the bound degrades by $\delta$
\citep{jaggi2013fw}.
\end{corollary}

\begin{remark}[Reading the prototype]
\label{rem:proto}
The reference prototype reweights with a softmax of the standardised
negative log-likelihood, $w_i\propto\exp(\eta\, z_i)$ with
$z_i=(\mathrm{nll}_i-\overline{\mathrm{nll}})/\mathrm{std}(\mathrm{nll})$,
and grid-searches $\alpha\in[0.02,0.6]$. Theorem~\ref{thm:fw} identifies
the \emph{exact} oracle as $w_i\propto1/p_{t-1}(z_i)=\exp(\mathrm{nll}_i)$:
the prototype's temperature-$\eta$, rank-stabilised softmax is a smoothed,
variance-controlled surrogate of the exact inverse-density weights (it
clips the heavy tail of $1/p$ that would otherwise let one outlier
dominate the resample). The grid search is a coarse exact-line-search.
Thus the prototype \emph{is} approximate Frank--Wolfe, and
Corollary~\ref{cor:rate} explains both its monotone training-LL ascent and
its diminishing returns per round. The principled replacements---exact
$w_i\propto1/p_{t-1}$ with curvature-aware clipping, and a Newton line
search or the closed-form FW step $\alpha_t=\min\{1,\,
D\mathcal{L}(p_{t-1})[c_t-p_{t-1}]/C_{\mathcal{L}}\}$---are what the library
implements.
\end{remark}

The upshot: generative boosting is bias reduction \emph{within} the
generative class. It does not leave $\Prob$, needs no normaliser, and
converges at the standard FW rate. The only modelling commitment is the
atom set $\mathcal{A}$ (here, greedy JPTs); richer atoms (Dirichlet-smoothed
or dependency-guided leaves) enlarge $\mathcal{A}$ and raise the attainable
$\mathcal{L}(p^\star)$.

\section{Discriminative boosting is conditional-likelihood ascent}
\label{sec:gbm}

Discriminative \jptboost{} runs the GBM recipe of \S\ref{sec:background}.
The key observation---which is what makes the comparison with
\S\ref{sec:likeboost} subtle---is that GBM is additive in a
\emph{different space}. We make precise the sense in which it, too,
maximises a likelihood.

\begin{proposition}[GBM $=$ MLE of an exponential-family conditional]
\label{prop:gbm-mle}
Let the conditional model be a single-parameter (per class) exponential
family with natural parameter $\eta=F(x)$ and sufficient statistic $T(y)$:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:expfam}
p(y\mid x;F)\;=\;h(y)\,\exp\!\big(F(x)^\top T(y)-A(F(x))\big),
\qquad A(\eta)=\log\!\int h(y)e^{\eta^\top T(y)}\,d\mu_\Y(y).
\end{equation}
Then minimising the GBM risk $R(F)=\frac1n\sum_i\ell(y_i,F(x_i))$ with the
negative-log-likelihood loss $\ell(y,F(x))=-\log p(y\mid x;F)$ is
\emph{maximum conditional-likelihood} estimation, and the GBM
pseudo-residual is the exponential-family score
\[
r_i \;=\; -\frac{\partial \ell(y_i,F(x_i))}{\partial F}
\;=\; T(y_i)-\E\big[T(y)\mid x_i;F\big]
\;=\; T(y_i)-\nabla A(F(x_i)).
\]
In particular:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.4em,topsep=2pt]
\item \textbf{Squared error} is the Gaussian conditional
$p(y\mid x)=\mathcal{N}(y;F(x),\sigma^2)$ with $T(y)=y$,
$A(\eta)=\eta^2/2$ (fixed $\sigma$); the pseudo-residual is the plain
residual $y_i-F(x_i)$.
\item \textbf{Log-loss / cross-entropy} is the categorical conditional with
softmax mean $p(y=k\mid x)=e^{F_k(x)}/\sum_j e^{F_j(x)}$, $T(y)=$
one-hot$(y)$, $A(\eta)=\log\sum_j e^{\eta_j}$; the pseudo-residual is
$\mathbf 1\{y_i=k\}-p(y=k\mid x_i)$ (LogitBoost).
\end{itemize}
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
$\ell=-\log p(y\mid x;F)=A(F(x))-F(x)^\top T(y)-\log h(y)$. Differentiating
in $F$, $\partial\ell/\partial F = \nabla A(F(x))-T(y)$ and $\nabla
A(\eta)=\E[T(y)\mid\eta]$ is the exponential-family mean map; the
pseudo-residual is its negative. The two special cases substitute the
stated $T,A$.
\end{proof}

The decisive structural fact in \eqref{eq:expfam} is that the additive
predictor $F=\sum_m\nu h_m$ enters as the \emph{natural parameter}
$\eta=F(x)$. Additivity in $\eta$ is \emph{not} additivity in the density:
by \eqref{eq:expfam},
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:tilt}
p(y\mid x;F_{m})\;\propto\;p(y\mid x;F_{m-1})\cdot
\exp\!\big(\nu\, h_m(x)^\top T(y)\big),
\end{equation}
so one boosting round \emph{multiplies} the conditional density by an
exponential tilt and \emph{re-normalises} by $A$. Function-space additive
boosting is, in density space, a \emph{product-of-experts} tilt---the exact
opposite of the convex mixture of \S\ref{sec:likeboost}.

\section{Reconciliation: two geometries, one principle, and the subsumption verdict}
\label{sec:reconcile}

We can now answer the central question of \S\ref{sec:intro}. Place the two
constructions side by side:

\begin{center}
\renewcommand{\arraystretch}{1.25}
\begin{tabular}{@{}lll@{}}
\toprule
 & \textbf{Generative \likeboost} & \textbf{Discriminative \jptboost} \\
\midrule
Objective & joint LL $\mathcal{L}(p)$ & conditional LL of $p(y\mid x)$ \\
Variable & density $p\in\Prob$ & natural parameter $F\in\mathcal{H}$ \\
Geometry & convex simplex of densities & affine function space \\
Atom combine & convex mixture $(1{-}\alpha)p+\alpha c$ & sum $F+\nu h$ \\
Density effect & mixture (``OR'' of experts) & exp.\ tilt (``AND''/product) \\
Normaliser & $Z\equiv 1$ (structural) & $A(F(x))$ over $y$ only \\
Output contract & full joint $p(x,y)$ & conditional $\E[y\mid x]$ \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

\paragraph{The tractability dichotomy.}
Why can GBM additively tilt at all, when the analogous additive tilt of a
\emph{joint} is the intractable product-of-experts of
Remark~\ref{rem:no-Z}? Because conditioning changes what the normaliser
integrates over.

\begin{theorem}[Tractability dichotomy of additive-tilt boosting]
\label{thm:dichotomy}
Consider boosting a density by additive natural-parameter updates
\eqref{eq:tilt}.
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\alph*),leftmargin=2em]
\item \textbf{Joint tilt is intractable.} Modelling the \emph{joint}
$p(x,y;F)\propto p_0(x,y)\exp(F(x,y))$ requires the partition function
$Z(F)=\int_{\X\times\Y}p_0\,e^{F}\,d\mu$, an integral over the entire data
space with no closed form for tree-structured $F$; this is the
product-of-experts normaliser.
\item \textbf{Conditional tilt is tractable.} Modelling the
\emph{conditional} $p(y\mid x;F)$ as in \eqref{eq:expfam} requires only the
\emph{per-$x$} log-partition $A(F(x))=\log\int_\Y h(y)e^{F(x)^\top T(y)}
d\mu_\Y(y)$, an integral/sum over the (low-dimensional) target $\Y$ alone,
closed-form for the Gaussian ($A=\eta^2/2$) and categorical
($A=\log\sum_j e^{\eta_j}$) heads.
\end{enumerate}
Hence additive-tilt boosting is tractable \emph{iff} the boosted parameter
feeds a conditional whose log-partition integrates over the target alone.
The generative mixture of \S\ref{sec:likeboost} sidesteps (a) entirely:
mixing normalised densities keeps $Z\equiv1$ (Rmk.~\ref{rem:no-Z}), at the
cost of expressing improvement as a mixture rather than a tilt.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
Both claims are the definition of the normalising constant of an
exponential tilt over the respective domain. In (a) the carrier is
$\X\times\Y$ and $F$ couples $x$ and $y$, so $Z(F)$ does not factor and is
not available in closed form for piecewise tree functions. In (b) the
tilt's free argument is $y$ with $x$ held fixed, so the normaliser is the
log-partition $A$ of the single-variable family \eqref{eq:expfam},
evaluated at $\eta=F(x)$; for the Gaussian and categorical families $A$ is
elementary. The generative claim is Proposition~\ref{prop:mixture-closed}.
\end{proof}

Theorem~\ref{thm:dichotomy} is the crux. It says discriminative boosting is
not a free lunch on top of the joint: it is tractable \emph{precisely
because} it gives up the joint and conditions, shrinking the normaliser
from an integral over $\X\times\Y$ to one over $\Y$. This is the formal
content of ``\jptboost{} trades away the joint.''

\paragraph{Is \jptboost{} a special case of \likeboost?}
The unification hypothesis asked whether feeding the ``conditional
objective'' to the generative mixture machinery reproduces GBM. It does
not, and the obstruction is geometric, not incidental.

\begin{proposition}[Non-subsumption]
\label{prop:nonsubsumption}
Let $\mathcal{M}=\{\sum_{t} w_t c_t : w\in\Delta,\ c_t\in\mathcal{A}\}$ be
the set of finite convex mixtures of atoms (the \likeboost{} hypothesis
class) and $\mathcal{T}=\{p_0\prod_t e^{\nu h_t^\top T}/Z\}$ the set of
additive-tilt / product models (the \jptboost{} hypothesis class). Then
$\mathcal{M}\neq\mathcal{T}$, and generically $\mathcal{T}\not\subseteq
\mathcal{M}$: a product/tilt of two distinct atom densities is not a finite
convex combination of those atoms. Consequently \jptboost{} is \emph{not} a
parametric special case of \likeboost.
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}[Proof sketch]
$\mathcal{M}$ is the convex hull of $\mathcal{A}$; it is closed under convex
combination and its extreme points are the atoms. $\mathcal{T}$ is closed
under \emph{multiplication} (addition of log-densities), with extreme
behaviour set by sums of $\log$-atoms. A normalised product
$p_0\,c_1 c_2/Z$ with $c_1,c_2$ supported on overlapping cells assigns
density proportional to $c_1(z)c_2(z)$, which is quadratic in the atom
values, whereas any element of $\mathcal{M}$ is affine in the atom values
$\{c_t(z)\}$; affine $\neq$ quadratic unless one factor is constant. Hence
the generic tilt lies outside $\mathcal{M}$.
\end{proof}

One might still hope that the \emph{predictor} of the generative booster
recovers the discriminative one---that $\E[y\mid x]$ ``falls out'' as a
conditional query on the boosted mixture. It falls out, but as a different
estimator:

\begin{proposition}[Conditioning a mixture is a gated mixture-of-experts]
\label{prop:moe}
Let $p_t(x,y)=\sum_{m} w_m\,c_m(x,y)$ be a \likeboost{} mixture. Its
conditional mean is the responsibility-weighted average of the
per-component conditional means,
\[
\E_{p_t}[y\mid x]\;=\;\sum_{m} r_m(x)\,\E_{c_m}[y\mid x],
\qquad r_m(x)=\frac{w_m\,c_m(x)}{\sum_{m'} w_{m'} c_{m'}(x)},
\]
a \emph{gated mixture-of-experts} regressor. This is structurally distinct
from the additive-logit GBM predictor $F(x)=\sum_m \nu h_m(x)$: the former
\emph{averages} experts with input-dependent gates summing to one; the
latter \emph{sums} stage-wise residual corrections without renormalisation.
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
$\E_{p_t}[y\mid x]=\int y\,p_t(x,y)\,dy / p_t(x)
=\sum_m w_m\!\int y\,c_m(x,y)\,dy / p_t(x)
=\sum_m \tfrac{w_m c_m(x)}{p_t(x)}\,\E_{c_m}[y\mid x]$, using
$\int y\,c_m(x,y)\,dy=c_m(x)\E_{c_m}[y\mid x]$. The gates $r_m(x)$ sum to
one by construction.
\end{proof}

Proposition~\ref{prop:moe} explains the empirical pattern in which the
discriminative booster wins on point accuracy: a gated average of greedy
conditional means is a weaker bias-reducer than additive residual
correction, because each expert is still a single tree's conditional mean
and the gates can only \emph{re-weight}, never \emph{add to}, them.
Bias that all experts share is invisible to a convex average but is exactly
what residual addition removes.

\paragraph{The unification that does hold.}
The two constructions \emph{are} two instances of one principle---just not
the one the naive hypothesis proposed. The shared object is greedy
\emph{functional} likelihood ascent.

\begin{definition}[Greedy functional likelihood ascent]
\label{def:metaboost}
Fix a likelihood functional $\mathcal{L}$, a geometry (a convex set or an
affine space) $\mathcal{G}$ with an inner product, and an atom family
$\mathcal{A}$. The greedy scheme iterates: \emph{(1)} compute the
functional gradient $\nabla\mathcal{L}$ at the current iterate; \emph{(2)}
call a linear oracle $s_t\in\argmax_{s\in\mathcal{A}}\inner{\nabla
\mathcal{L}}{s}$; \emph{(3)} combine $\theta_t = \theta_{t-1}\oplus
\gamma_t s_t$ with the combine operator $\oplus$ of $\mathcal{G}$ and a
line-/shrinkage step $\gamma_t$.
\end{definition}

\noindent\likeboost{} is Definition~\ref{def:metaboost} with
$\mathcal{L}=$ joint LL, $\mathcal{G}=$ the density simplex, $\oplus=$
convex combination, oracle $=$ inverse-density reweighting
(Thm.~\ref{thm:fw}). \jptboost{} is Definition~\ref{def:metaboost} with
$\mathcal{L}=$ conditional LL, $\mathcal{G}=$ the affine
natural-parameter space, $\oplus=$ addition, oracle $=$ least-squares fit
to the score / pseudo-residual (Prop.~\ref{prop:gbm-mle}). One principle;
two objectives; two geometries; two combine operators.

\begin{corollary}[Design verdict]
\label{cor:verdict}
There is no single parametric boosting class with an ``objective'' flag
that flips \likeboost{} into \jptboost{}: the two differ in geometry and
combine operator, not merely in a loss
(Prop.~\ref{prop:nonsubsumption}). They share an \emph{interface}---greedy
functional likelihood ascent over a \mixture{} of JPTs
(Def.~\ref{def:metaboost})---but are realised as \emph{sibling classes}.
Bagging (\S\ref{sec:bagging}) is a third member of the interface: a
non-greedy, equal-weight mixture (no oracle, no line search). The
generative members (\jptforest, \likeboost) keep the joint; the
discriminative member (\jptboost) is documented as conditional and gives it
up.
\end{corollary}

\section{Architectural consequences}
\label{sec:arch}

Corollary~\ref{cor:verdict} maps directly onto the library. All ensembles
are mixtures of JPTs (Prop.~\ref{prop:mixture-closed}), so a single base
type carries the query surface and serialisation; the three constructions
are thin training strategies on top.

\begin{description}[leftmargin=1.2em,style=nextline]
\item[\mixture{} (base).] Holds members $\{p^{(m)}\}$ and weights $w$.
Delegates the full query surface by
Proposition~\ref{prop:mixture-closed}: \texttt{posterior}/\texttt{expectation}
merge per-member leaf distributions of the queried type with (evidence-
adjusted) weights via the type's \texttt{merge}; \texttt{likelihood}/
\texttt{pdf} are the weighted sum of member densities;
\texttt{infer} combines member joints; \texttt{sample} picks a member by
weight then samples it; \texttt{mpe} is approximate (per-member MPE,
re-scored under the mixture). JSON/pickle mirror \texttt{JPT}.
\item[\jptforest{} (bagging, generative).] Equal weights, bootstrap
members, parallel training via the existing \texttt{multicore.Pool}
(members are independent; the C4.5 module-global \texttt{threading.local}
state means members must train across \emph{processes}, not threads).
Variance reduction (Prop.~\ref{prop:bag-var}); keeps the joint.
\item[\likeboost{} (generative boosting).] Frank--Wolfe of
Theorem~\ref{thm:fw}: inverse-density reweighting oracle (exact
$w_i\propto1/p_{t-1}$, curvature-clipped per Rmk.~\ref{rem:proto}) and a
line-searched / closed-form $\alpha_t$. Stopping when the FW gap
$D\mathcal{L}(p_{t-1})[c_t-p_{t-1}]$ falls below tolerance or held-out joint
LL stalls. Keeps the joint.
\item[\jptboost{} (discriminative boosting).] Stage-wise additive GBM
(Prop.~\ref{prop:gbm-mle}): squared error (regression) first, then
categorical/softmax (LogitBoost) as the natural extension. Documented
explicitly as \emph{conditional}: it estimates $\E[y\mid x]$ and forfeits
the generative joint (Thm.~\ref{thm:dichotomy}). It can be \emph{exposed}
through \mixture{} only as the gated mixture-of-experts of
Proposition~\ref{prop:moe} when a joint is required; its native output is
the additive predictor.
\end{description}

Two cross-cutting refinements enlarge the atom family $\mathcal{A}$ and so
raise the attainable optimum of Theorem~\ref{thm:fw} and the quality of
every member: \emph{Dirichlet-smoothed} leaf multinomials (an $\alpha$
prior, better-calibrated members; cf.\ the log-loss collapse without
smoothing) and \emph{native C4.5 sample weights} (Cython work in
\texttt{impurity.pyx} mirroring the existing validation mask) so that
boosting and bagging can weight rather than only resample---resampling is
the Monte-Carlo approximation to the exact weighted oracle.

\section{Preliminary empirical evidence}
\label{sec:experiments}

The theory predicts three things, each already visible in the prototype
study that motivated this work (single $75/25$ split, seed $0$;
\emph{to be reproduced from the library API} in the implementation phases).
We report them as preliminary corroboration, not as the paper's final
experiments.

\paragraph{(P1) Bagging reduces variance, keeps the joint.}
Averaging $15$ bootstrap JPTs (Prop.~\ref{prop:bag-var}):
\texttt{wine} accuracy $0.867\!\to\!0.978$, \texttt{digits}
$0.762\!\to\!0.869$, \texttt{diabetes} $R^2\,{-}0.029\!\to\!0.267$,
\texttt{abalone} $0.425\!\to\!0.463$. Never harmful here; sometimes past
LightGBM---consistent with the gap being removable variance, not a
representational ceiling.

\paragraph{(P2) Generative boosting improves held-out joint LL, staying
generative.} \likeboost{} (Frank--Wolfe, $8$ rounds, line-searched
$\alpha$) lifts mean held-out \emph{joint} log-likelihood on every dataset:
\texttt{diabetes} $-7.23\!\to\!+5.88$, \texttt{iris}
$-15.14\!\to\!-5.73$, \texttt{abalone} $+10.22\!\to\!+11.36$, \texttt{wine}
$-27.36\!\to\!-26.93$. The result is still a normalised joint
(Thm.~\ref{thm:fw}), so $p(x)$ anomaly scoring and arbitrary queries carry
over.

\paragraph{(P3) Discriminative boosting wins point accuracy, gives up the
joint.} \jptboost{} (squared error, $40$ rounds, $\nu=0.1$) reaches
\texttt{diabetes} $R^2\,0.267$ and \texttt{abalone} $0.551$, the latter
past LightGBM's $0.518$---bias reduction by additive residual correction
that the gated average of Proposition~\ref{prop:moe} does not match, at the
documented cost of the joint (Thm.~\ref{thm:dichotomy}).

These corroborate the geometry: bagging touches variance, generative
boosting raises the joint likelihood within $\Prob$, discriminative
boosting raises conditional accuracy outside it. The implementation phases
reproduce each number through the public API, add held-out FW-gap and
calibration curves, and extend P3 to classification (LogitBoost).

\section{Related work}
\label{sec:related}

\textbf{Density / generative boosting.}
Boosting density estimates as additive mixtures has a long lineage:
\citet{rosset2002boostdensity} boost density estimates by likelihood;
\citet{tolstikhin2017adagan} (AdaGAN) and \citet{grover2018boosted}
re-derive greedy additive generative mixtures; the convex-combination view
as Frank--Wolfe over the marginal-polytope / density simplex is made
explicit by \citet{cranko2019boosting} and connects to the
mixture-density and gradient-boosting-of-distributions literature. Our
Theorem~\ref{thm:fw} instantiates this for tree-structured \emph{joint}
densities with mixed variable types and identifies the inverse-density
reweighting as the exact oracle. \textbf{Gradient boosting.}
\citet{friedman2001gbm} and the functional-gradient view of
\citet{mason2000boosting} underlie \jptboost; modern scalable
implementations \citep{chen2016xgboost,ke2017lightgbm} are the empirical
yardstick. \textbf{Probabilistic / generative trees and forests.}
Bagging and random forests \citep{breiman1996bagging,breiman2001rf};
density forests and generative forests \citep{criminisi2012decision,
correia2020joints}; sum-product networks and probabilistic circuits
\citep{poon2011spn,choi2020pc}, with which a mixture of JPTs shares the
``decomposable/deterministic mixture'' tractability story and to which our
self-normalisation argument (Prop.~\ref{prop:jpt-norm}) is closely related.
\textbf{JPTs.} The base formalism and its query semantics are due to
\citet{nyga2023jpt}; this paper supplies the ensemble theory and the
generative-vs-discriminative reconciliation it left open.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We set out to decide, by mathematics, whether one likelihood-boosting
framework subsumes both the generative and the discriminative ensembling of
JPTs. The answer is a qualified no: the two are unified by a single
meta-principle---greedy functional ascent of a likelihood via a linear
oracle on its functional gradient (Def.~\ref{def:metaboost})---but they act
in two irreconcilable geometries. Generative likelihood boosting is exact
Frank--Wolfe over the density simplex, a self-normalised convex mixture
that keeps the joint (Thm.~\ref{thm:fw}); discriminative boosting is
maximum conditional-likelihood estimation with an additive natural
parameter, tractable only because conditioning shrinks an otherwise
intractable joint partition function to a sum over the target
(Thm.~\ref{thm:dichotomy}). A conditioned generative mixture is a gated
mixture-of-experts, not the additive-logit predictor
(Prop.~\ref{prop:moe}), so discriminative \jptboost{} is not a parametric
special case of generative \likeboost{} (Prop.~\ref{prop:nonsubsumption}).
The design follows: a single \mixture{} base carrying the query surface,
with \jptforest, \likeboost{} and \jptboost{} as sibling strategies under a
shared interface---bagging and likelihood boosting keeping the generative
joint, \jptboost{} documented as the discriminative configuration that
trades it for accuracy. The implementation phases realise this API and
reproduce the preliminary numbers of \S\ref{sec:experiments} from the
public library.
